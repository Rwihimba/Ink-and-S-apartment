If you're staying in Kigali for more than a couple of nights, the "apartment or hotel?" question is really a "what am I actually paying for?" question. Here's an honest breakdown of where the money goes — and when a furnished apartment wins.

## The headline number isn't the whole story

A hotel's nightly rate looks simple, but the real cost of a stay adds up across a few lines:

- **Breakfast** — often charged per person, per day at hotels; included with us.
- **WiFi** — usually free at both, but apartment fibre tends to be faster and less congested.
- **Laundry** — hotel laundry is priced per item and adds up fast on longer trips. An apartment lets you do your own.
- **Meals** — hotels nudge you toward eating out three times a day; with us a fresh breakfast is already included, trimming a chunk off your daily food spend.
- **Space** — a hotel room is one room; a furnished apartment gives you a separate living area and a dedicated workspace for the same kind of money.

## Where a furnished apartment pulls ahead

The longer you stay, the more the maths tilts toward an apartment:

1. **Nightly rates drop for weekly and monthly stays.** Direct-booked apartments almost always offer long-stay discounts that hotels rarely match.
2. **Breakfast is included.** A fresh breakfast every morning is on the house — one less meal to buy each day of your stay.
3. **No per-item extras.** Laundry, breakfast and "resort" fees don't nickel-and-dime you.
4. **One booking covers a group.** A living area and sofa can host colleagues or family without a second room.

> Rule of thumb: for one or two nights, a hotel is fine. From about three nights up — and especially for weekly or monthly stays — a furnished apartment usually costs less and gives you far more space.

## Where a hotel still makes sense

- Ultra-short stays (a single night near the airport).
- Trips where you specifically want daily housekeeping, a restaurant downstairs and a front desk at 2am.
